Does the National Gallery of Art Museum use data management and storage techniques that allow for the preservation and accessibility of digital objects?

Based on our data analysis and cleaning several datasets included in the National Art Museum's ‘opendata’ repository, we would say that the National Art Museum's data curation methods meet general data management standards that allow for the preservation and accessibility of digital objects. The National Art Museum provided a data dictionary in their repository as well as a relational model and extensive SQL code. All of these features made analysis easier. Additionally, the datasets are updated regularly, usually every day, which means that the timeliness of this data is strong.

There were certain elements that hindered the usefulness and accessibility of the data, such as there being a significant amount of missing values and many duplicate values that were misclassified in the objects dataset. Because of this, we had to do additional data cleaning methods to merge categories that were misspelled or capitalized incorrectly. Furthermore, the missing values were labeled in the csv files in inconsistent ways. In some csv files missing values were ‘0’s, in other files they were ‘NaN’. The artwork in the Objects dataset include digital media, but looking at the Media dataset specifically allows for further analysis of the digital object curation practices of the National Galley of Art. For example, the links provided in the Media dataset are for internal use at the National Art Museum. When using the requests library to determine the status code of each url listed in the thumnail, play, download, and image url columns, all requests resulted in an error. Most of these errors code were 200, 401, 403, or 404 errors indicating that the request either failed or succeeded but the site data could not be properly accessed. For the download urls, a significant amount (46.7%) of requests resulted in a 429 error which indicates that the request failed because too many requests were sent to the server. Due to the internal nature of these links, people using the data outside of the National Gallery of Art will be required to do additional searching, likely on the National Art Museum’s website, to gain further information on the objects. The accessibility of digital media is a factor in its popularity, as many people can create and view digital art no matter where they are. From an accessibility standpoint, the urls being for internal use only hinder dataset users' abilities to further connect with an understand the digital media. However, the National Gallery of Art is still preserving the digital media files by hosting them on their website. Although being able to directly access the media files from the dataset would be useful, the National Gallery of Art must also protect their artworks in terms of copyright and licesning so it is understandable that the media is stored in this way.

**Summary:** 

   In the digital age, the rise of digital media has made art easier to create and distribute. This poses an opportunity for museums to incorporate digital media into their catalogues, which blends traditional art curation methods with contemporary data archival practices. Therefore, our goal for this project is to analyze the historical and current practices of the National Gallery of Art Museum to gain a better understanding of the trends in art curation practices in this country. We will do this by combining various datasets provided by the Museum and analyzing them using SQL. The three of us worked together to draft a project plan and determine how we would split up the work, starting with analyzing our data and then answering our research questions based on our interpretations. There are several research questions that we will focus on to address our overall project goal: analyzing the historical and current practices of the National Gallery of Art Museum to gain a better understanding of the trends in art curation practices in the United States. The research questions we wanted to explore are:

1) Does the National Gallery of Art Museum use data management and storage techniques that allow for the preservation and accessibility of digital objects?
2) What is the difference between how digital and traditional media are treated in the National Gallery of Art Museum? Are there more digital items or traditional items?
3) We want to know what the relationship is between older, traditional works of art and modern works at the National Gallery of Art Museum in terms of what region they come from and the artist who made them.
   
Overall, we want to know what the relationship is between older, traditional works of art and modern works at the National Gallery of Art Museum in terms of what region they come from and the artist who made them. After answering our first research question, we found that the National Gallery of Art Museum provided a data dictionary in their repository as well as a relational model and extensive SQL code, which made analysis significantly easier. However, we had to perform additional data cleaning methods to merge categories that were misspelled, categorized incorrectly, and had typos. Additionally, we had to do additional searching on the National Gallery of Art Museum’s website to gain further information on what the digital objects were from the Media CSV file. We saw this as the main drawback to the ‘opendata’ repository since certain links provided in the Media CSV were only for internal use at the National Gallery of Art Museum.
When answering our first and second research questions, we answered this question by first using SQL to link the appropriate tables together. After doing this, we identified there were several null items in tables like the media_df table. After cleaning this data, we found that there was a disproportionate amount of digital media represented in the National Gallery of Art, as this was significantly underrepresented compared to the traditional media works in the Museum.

When answering the rest of our research questions, the first thing we did was look through datasets to see which had the relevant information, namely some kind of location value, an artist name, and a date created or donated. **Data profile:** 

   The first dataset, which is the objects CSV, is located in the data and museum_db_export file in the GitHub folder, features several variables such as object ID, accession number, location ID, title, display date, etc., and demonstrates descriptions of each of the artworks displayed in the museum, with descriptions of what each of the artworks represents. In general, the datasets we’re working with are all located in the museum_db_export file. When looking at the objects CSV file specifically, the first artwork represented in the dataset is a painting of Artaud de Montor, which is described in this dataset as “a Canvas of a diplomat in Rome.” Therefore, this specific dataset is useful for us because it provides us more information about each specific artwork with descriptions of what the paintings like in this case is portraying. Despite there being a lot of European paintings represented in this dataset, all of its descriptions and additional details are in English, which makes it easier for us to analyze the data as we are provided with the appropriate information about the historical contexts behind each of the paintings and their origins. However, some of the issues we found were that there were certain elements that hindered the usefulness and accessibility of the data, such as there being a significant amount of missing values and many duplicate values that were misclassified. Some of the ethical and legal constraints this raises is the lack of transparency and accountability when it comes to this dataset hiding this missing information because we weren’t sure why certain values were missing and what to do with the values labeled as “null” or “NA”. However, for this dataset, we would say that it was extremely useful and helpful to answer our research questions because we were able to use sql to create the digital objects database. This helped us look at the different media types and how the digital and traditional media are treated in the National Gallery of Art Museum by seeing whether there’s more digital or traditional media represented.

**Data cleaning:** 

As mentioned above, the main quality issues we found in all of our datasets were a large amount of missing values as well as a lack of consistency with labeling. To start our data cleaning, we did a dftypes statement for every database we imported into Visual Studio Code. We did this to see what type of values were stored in each database, which was important information to keep in mind when doing any sorting or dropping of values. The first dataset we cleaned was the media relationships dataset, which we found to have no missing values when running the Python code. The next dataset we cleaned was the media CSV file, which had a small amount of missing values but was inconsequential compared to further datasets. We also checked the types of values in the media dataset and found that it was mostly objects with one float and one integer value, duration, and mediaid, respectively. Next, we went on to cleaning the objects dataset. As mentioned above, this was the dataset with the most missing values. To start, the types of all the entries were either objects, integers, or floats. Categories such as objectid and iud had no missing values, but others had more than 100,000 missing. LocationId had 142,939 missing values. Displaydate had 11,514. Markings had 130,510, subclassification had 103,107, and so on. It would be pedantic to list every amount of missing values in this report, but roughly half of the categories in the dataset had at least 10,000 values missing. Part of the cleaning process for this dataset was changing these missing values to ‘unknown’, which would make it easier for analysis down the line. We also took the liberty of dropping categories that we knew were unrelated to our research question. For the objects dataset, for example, we dropped beginyear, endyear, creditline, and visualbrowsertimespan. Another cleaning process we did was adding ‘Not Linked’ for missing values under wikidataid. We followed similar processes for the rest of our datasets. For object constituents, we replaced 0’s under objectid with ‘NaN’, which would make it easier to sort later. Objectid was the category with the most missing values, so after making that change, we were able to have a relatively clean dataset with object, int, and float types. For the constituents dataset, more aggressive changes had to be made. For example, there were quite a few phrases used to describe an anonymous artist. Sometimes the placeholder was ‘anonymous artist,’ and sometimes it was ‘anonymous. We took the time to standardize this for processing. We ran Python code on two different categories in particular: preferred display name and forward display name. We made all of the entries lowercase and then replaced them with ‘Anonymous’. I mentioned previously in this section that most of our types were objects, integers, or floats. We did come across some strings, which we made an effort to change when creating our DuckDB tables. When we were setting all of the values, we made sure they were each one of the three aforementioned types above. This would help with standardization and, later down the line, analysis. 

Despite the fact that there were missing values, rather than ignoring these, we decided to adapt to these circumstances by adjusting our visualizations and analysis based on the discrepancies we noticed. When it came to answering the third question about the relationship between older, traditional works of art and modern works at the National Gallery of Art Museum, based on the region they come from and the artist who made them, we noticed that this regional term was rather ambiguous in the dataset because the only region we saw that was common was the “United Kingdom.” We had to account for all these missing values when it came to understanding the region that the artists came from, and this motivated us to move in a new direction when answering this question. We decided to reframe this question by answering it in terms of which artists were featured the most across various time periods. Overall, while there were several missing values in some of our datasets that required additional data cleaning, instead of forgetting about these missing values, we accounted for them and used them to our advantage when reframing how we would answer our research question.
